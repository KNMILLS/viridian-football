# Comprehensive Offensive and Defensive Schemes in American Football

This document summarises the principal offensive and defensive philosophies used in American football.  Each entry lists the core concepts, typical personnel requirements and any tactical nuances.  Citations are provided where available from coaching literature and analysis articles.

## 1 Offensive Philosophies

Modern offenses borrow concepts from multiple systems, but most can trace their lineage to a handful of foundational philosophies.  These schemes differ in how they attack a defense—some emphasise short passes and ball control, others vertical shots or power running.  The scheme descriptions below draw from coaching articles and Game analyses.

### 1.1 West Coast offense

The West Coast offense is a horizontal passing system that uses short and intermediate throws to stretch the defense sideline‑to‑sideline.  Bill Walsh designed it for quarterbacks who lacked a big arm but excelled at accuracy and timing.  By treating the short passing Game as an extension of the run, the offense seeks high completion rates and yards after catch【842711003000890†L49-L70】.  It relies on precise route‑running by possession receivers and tight ends, and a quarterback capable of quick reads【842711003000890†L96-L104】.  The run Game typically features power and iso plays【842711003000890†L78-L90】.

### 1.2 Air Coryell / Vertical offense

Named for coach Don Coryell, the vertical offense (often called “Air Coryell”) attacks the deep middle of the field with timed deep drops and vertical routes.  Receivers stretch the coverage horizontally and vertically, forcing safeties to choose between multiple seams【355019842974166†L36-L59】.  Quarterbacks must have strong arms and patience, as plays take longer to develop.  This philosophy often pairs with shot plays and play‑action.

### 1.3 Erhardt–Perkins system

The Erhardt–Perkins offense is a flexible, pro‑style system originating with Patriots coach Ron Erhardt and coordinator Ray Perkins.  It emphasises a powerful run Game to set up play‑action, uses a complex, adaptable vocabulary and allows plays to be altered at the line based on defensive alignment.  Quarterbacks in this system must be cerebral, and running backs must excel in pass protection and receiving.  While not in our citations, this philosophy underpins many modern “multiple” offenses in the NFL.

### 1.4 Spread offense and variants

The spread offense is a family of schemes that widen the formation with four or more receivers to create space for both runs and passes.  It is often run at an up‑tempo pace and allows quarterbacks to call plays at the line【462284417332835†L33-L45】.  Variants include:

* **Air Raid** – emphasises wide splits, four receivers and deep vertical routes.  Receivers initially run fly routes before breaking off【462284417332835†L128-L168】.
* **Spread Option** – pairs spread formations with zone‑read and quarterback run concepts, requiring a mobile quarterback.
* **Smashmouth Spread** – blends spread formations with power‑running plays to create inside and outside threats.
* **Pro‑Style Spread** – incorporates under‑center and shotgun looks for a balanced attack.

### 1.5 Option offense

Option schemes rely on the quarterback reading a specific defender to determine whether to hand the ball off, keep it or pitch it.  Variants include the **triple option** (dive, keep, pitch), the **veer option** (quick‑hitting at a sharp angle) and the **flexbone** (utilising three running backs).  Option football requires disciplined linemen and quarterbacks with good decision‑making and toughness.  Although the citations in this file do not cover option schemes directly, their influence is widespread in college football.

### 1.6 Run and Shoot offense

Developed in the 1960s and popularised by the Houston Oilers and University of Hawaii, the Run and Shoot is a ball‑control passing attack that features four receiver sets and a shotgun quarterback.  Receivers run option routes and adjust their patterns based on coverage【296463773246717†L82-L88】.  The scheme spreads the defense and requires an accurate quarterback who can read and react quickly【296463773246717†L44-L49】.  It is not a quick‑strike offense but uses high‑percentage passes to methodically move the ball【296463773246717†L65-L72】.

### 1.7 Power run / Smashmouth football

Smashmouth offenses prioritise a strong downhill running Game behind a dominant offensive line.  The goal is to control the line of scrimmage and wear down the defense with powerful backs.  Passing is used sparingly to keep the defense honest.  The scheme emphasises physicality and often features fullbacks and blocking tight ends.  While no specific citation is available here, the power run approach is the antithesis of spread and vertical philosophies.

### 1.8 Other formations and systems

* **Pro‑style offense** – A balanced run/pass attack using multiple formations and personnel groupings.  It relies on complex reads and versatile players.  Strong‑armed quarterbacks and do‑it‑all running backs thrive【254128993658019†L39-L51】【254128993658019†L61-L74】.
* **Pistol alignment** – A hybrid of shotgun and single‑back looks.  The quarterback lines up 4–5 yards behind the center with a running back directly behind him, allowing downhill running while preserving shotgun passing advantages【524210843513336†L44-L58】.  Coach Chris Ault created the pistol to combine elements of pro‑style and spread systems【524210843513336†L67-L72】.
* **Wishbone formation** – An option‑based formation featuring a fullback and two halfbacks behind the quarterback.  It emphasises power running, misdirection and balanced line play【362565299932892†L185-L195】【362565299932892†L198-L207】.  Counters, traps and blast plays are common【362565299932892†L223-L231】.
* **I‑formation** – A classic formation with a fullback and tailback lined up behind the quarterback.  It supports both power running and play‑action passing.
* **T‑formation** – An early football formation with three backs in a straight line behind the quarterback.  Used in historical offenses.
* **Single Wing** – A direct‑snap formation from football’s early days, emphasising misdirection and blocking angles.  Rare in modern football.
* **Wildcat** – A variation of the shotgun where a running back or receiver takes the snap.  It aims to create a numerical advantage in the run Game and is often used as a trick package.

## 2 Defensive Philosophies

Defenses are defined by their “fronts” (distribution of linemen and linebackers) and coverage schemes.  Modern defenses mix fronts and coverages to counter varied offenses.

### 2.1 Base fronts

* **4‑3 defense** – A balanced front with four down linemen and three linebackers.  Each front‑seven defender is responsible for one gap, making it effective against both run and pass【630393624974907†L23-L41】.  The scheme allows a variety of zone and man coverages【630393624974907†L58-L69】.  It requires strong defensive ends, run‑stuffing tackles and versatile linebackers.
* **3‑4 defense** – Comprises three down linemen and four linebackers.  It hides the fourth rusher, creating unpredictability in the pass rush【167553913613363†L24-L46】.  Each defender has one‑gap responsibilities and the scheme is flexible for blitz packages【167553913613363†L87-L99】.  A dominant nose tackle anchors the line, while hybrid linebackers bring pressure and cover.
* **5‑2 defense** – Five defensive linemen and two linebackers.  Designed to stop power running attacks by clogging the line of scrimmage.  Often used in goal‑line situations.
* **4‑4 defense** – Four linemen and four linebackers.  This “4‑4 split” is a run‑focused front to counter heavy rushing offenses.
* **3‑3‑5 defense** – Also called the “Stack,” with three linemen, three linebackers and five defensive backs.  The stacked alignment disguises who will rush and who will cover, making it effective against spread offenses.
* **4‑2‑5 defense (Nickel)** – A variation of the 4‑3 where one linebacker is replaced with a fifth defensive back.  This is a standard response to three‑receiver sets.  The nickel corner must be agile and strong in run support【213508930002408†L70-L97】.
* **Dime and Quarter defenses** – Packages with six or seven defensive backs respectively.  Used in obvious passing situations to prevent deep completions.
* **46 defense** – A high‑pressure scheme with eight men in the box, pioneered by Buddy Ryan.  It aims to overwhelm the offense with blitzes and stop the run at all costs.  The “46” aligns the strong safety and both linebackers near the line.

### 2.2 Coverage shells

* **Cover 0** – Pure man‑to‑man coverage with no deep safety help.  Often accompanied by a heavy blitz.  High risk, high reward.
* **Cover 1 (Man‑Free)** – Man coverage with a single free safety deep.  Allows aggressive press coverage and safety help where needed.
* **Cover 2** – Zone coverage with two deep safeties dividing the deep field and five underneath defenders covering flats, hooks and curls【803635243678639†L67-L71】.  Corners jam receivers then drop to flats【803635243678639†L75-L83】.  Linebackers must “pattern read” and carry vertical routes through their zones【803635243678639†L90-L99】.  The Tampa 2 variant drops the middle linebacker deep to protect the seam【803635243678639†L103-L124】.
* **Cover 3** – Three deep defenders (two corners and a free safety) each cover a deep third, with four underneath zone defenders.  Effective against vertical routes but susceptible to quick flats.
* **Cover 4 (Quarters)** – Four deep defenders each cover a quarter of the field.  Strong against deep passes and vertical routes.
* **Cover 6** – A hybrid of Cover 2 and Cover 4; one side plays quarters, the other plays Cover 2.  Useful against trips formations.
* **Zone Blitz** – A pressure package where a lineman drops into zone coverage and a linebacker or defensive back blitzes.  Confuses the offensive line’s blocking assignments.
* **Pattern Matching** – A hybrid coverage where defenders begin in zone but switch to man coverage based on receiver routes.  Combines zone vision with man coverage tightness.

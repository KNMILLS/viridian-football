# ADR-0003: Injury Risk Model
**Document ID**: ADR-0003  
**Version**: 1.0  
**Last Updated**: 2024-12-19  
**Status**: Active

## Context

The USE Engine requires a realistic injury risk modeling system that accurately represents the probability of player injuries based on contact severity, body positioning, fatigue, and environmental factors. The injury system must integrate with body state tracking, fatigue modeling, and CPU GM decision-making to provide authentic gameplay that reflects real-world football injury patterns.

Key forces at play:
- **Realism vs. Enjoyment**: Injuries must be realistic without making the game frustrating
- **Complexity vs. Predictability**: Detailed injury modeling vs. player ability to understand and manage risk
- **Contact vs. Non-contact**: Different injury mechanisms require different modeling approaches
- **Individual vs. Population**: Injury risk varies by player but must align with league-wide statistics
- **Short-term vs. Long-term**: Acute injuries vs. chronic conditions and cumulative damage

The research emphasizes the importance of player development, morale, injuries, and fatigue realism【message_idx†source】, requiring an injury system that supports dynamic player narratives and realistic team management decisions.

## Decision

We will implement a logistic hazard model with context multipliers:

1. **Base Injury Rate**: Position-specific baseline injury probability
2. **Contact Energy Hazard**: Logistic function of collision energy and impact angle
3. **Body State Multipliers**: Risk modification based on unsafe head position, awkward body angles
4. **Fatigue Coupling**: Increased susceptibility when players are fatigued
5. **Context Multipliers**: Field position, surface conditions, weather effects
6. **Injury Taxonomy**: Categorized injury types with severity distributions and recovery curves

The model will use logistic regression with multiplicative context factors to ensure realistic injury probabilities while maintaining computational efficiency.

## Alternatives

**Alternative 1: Simple Probability Model**
- Description: Use fixed injury probabilities for different contact types
- Pros: Simple implementation, easy to understand and balance
- Cons: Unrealistic, doesn't account for context, poor integration with other systems
- Why Rejected: Insufficient realism for authentic football simulation

**Alternative 2: Complex Biomechanical Model**
- Description: Implement detailed biomechanical modeling of injury mechanisms
- Pros: Maximum realism, scientifically accurate
- Cons: Extremely complex, requires extensive data, performance overhead
- Why Rejected: Overly complex for current requirements and team capabilities

**Alternative 3: Statistical Model Only**
- Description: Use purely statistical models based on historical NFL injury data
- Pros: Data-driven, potentially accurate for population-level statistics
- Cons: Requires extensive data, may not capture individual variation, black-box behavior
- Why Rejected: Lack of comprehensive data and need for explainable, individual player modeling

**Alternative 4: Event-Based Injury System**
- Description: Injuries occur only from specific high-impact events
- Pros: Clear cause-effect relationships, easy to balance
- Cons: Unrealistic, misses cumulative damage and non-contact injuries
- Why Rejected: Incomplete modeling of real-world injury patterns

**Alternative 5: Deterministic Injury System**
- Description: Injuries occur when specific thresholds are exceeded
- Pros: Predictable, easy to test and validate
- Cons: Unrealistic, removes uncertainty, poor gameplay experience
- Why Rejected: Injuries must have realistic uncertainty and randomness

## Consequences

**Positive Consequences:**
- **Realism**: Logistic hazard model accurately reflects real-world injury probability patterns
- **Integration**: Context multipliers enable realistic interaction with body states and fatigue
- **Flexibility**: Multiplicative factors allow for complex risk scenarios
- **Validation**: Explicit formulas enable comprehensive testing and validation
- **Scalability**: Efficient computation supports full team simulation

**Negative Consequences:**
- **Complexity**: Multiplicative model requires careful parameter tuning
- **Balance**: Injury rates must be carefully balanced to maintain enjoyable gameplay
- **Validation**: Complex model requires extensive testing across different scenarios
- **Uncertainty**: Random nature of injuries may frustrate some players

**Neutral Consequences:**
- **Learning Curve**: Team must understand injury model parameters and effects
- **Maintenance**: Injury formulas may need adjustment based on gameplay feedback
- **Documentation**: Comprehensive documentation required for injury system
